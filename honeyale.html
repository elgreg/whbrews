Honey Ale

Ingredients

Makes 5 gallons of beer

Sanitize all your equipment before brewing. If you're using the recommended yeast, Wyeast, you'll want to smack your yeast packet now and let it sit out of the fridge to warm up a little.

Grains

  • 8 lbs Maris Otter
  • 0.75 lb Belgian Biscuit
  • 0.5 lb Crystal 40L

Hops

  • 1.5 oz Kent Goldings 45
  • 1.5 oz Fuggles 1

Yeast

  • 1335 Wyeast British Ale II

Mash

We're making a lovely concoction of water and grains to extract all the good sugars and enzymes from the malted barley. It should smell delicious and amazing.

Things you'll need for this step

  • A really big pot
  • A picnic cooler fitted with a valve and a flase bottom
  • A really long spoon or stirring utensil
  • A thermometer
  • Your grains

If you want to get crafty, use this calculator to figure out your strike temperature (or how high you have to heat the water before adding the grains)

If you don't have a picnic cooler with a false bottom, you can use your big pot, but you'll want to keep an eye on the the target temperature, 152 degrees, and stir every now and then. You'll also need another pot to heat up more water.

1. Step One

Heat up 3 gallons of water to 164°F (this is your strike temperature).

2. Step Two

Add the grains and water to the cooler and mix. Secure the top back on the cooler, then start the mash timer.

Start the 60 Minute Mash

Oh hey, you'll want to heat up 5.49 gallons of water for the next step right about now.

Sparge

1. Step One

Pour the water you've been heating up in your big pot into the cooler to raise the temperature up past 172°F.

2. Step Two

Recirculate wort (this is now what your liquid is called) from the valve at the bottom of the cooler to the top of the cooler. Do this for a while till the wort gets a bit clearer.

3. Step Three

Attach one end of tubing to the valve and the aerator to the other end. Let the wort flow out of the cooler into the big pot. Gravity is amazing.

Boil

1. Step One

Put the pot on the stove and heat it up to a boil. It should start to foam on top, just keep an eye on it for when the boil breaks, as you don't want the pot to boil over.

2. Step Two

Reduce the temperature to a steady boil. Now you can start the timer for the boil.

Start the 60 Minute Boil

Post-Boil

Things you'll need

  • A wort chiller (or an ice bath)
  • A fine mesh strainer
  • A 5 gallon carboy
  • Your yeast
  • A stopper and some tubing
  • A jar filled with some sanitizer. (Enough to cover the end of the tube)
  • A funnel

1. Step One

Cool down your beer to 76°F - 73°F. No more, no less. (Refer to your yeast packaging if you're not using the recommended yeast.)

2. Step Two

Pour the chilled wort into your carboy through the funnel with the strainer overtop of the funnel to catch any stray clumps of hops.

3. Step Three

Add your yeast to the carboy, cover the hole and shake everything up!

4. Step Four

Attach bung and tubing to the top of the carboy. Put the other end of the tubing into the jar with the sanitizer and put it in a safe place. Try to keep your carboy at a temperature between 63°F and 75°F.

Ferment

1. Step One

We're going to let this ale sit for a total of three weeks. So you're going to just have to wait.

2. Step Two

After your yeast calms down a bit (usually two or three days), replace the tubing with your airlock (half filled with sanitizer or course).

Bottling

Sometimes you want to bottle, sometimes you want to keg. I'm assuming you'd like to bottle this beer.

Things you'll need

  • A racking cane and tubing
  • A really big pot!
  • 3.54 ounces of White House honey (or just your own honey if you're not neighbors)
  • A sanitized measuring cup and spoon
  • A little bit of water to dissolve the honey in
  • About 50 bottles of sanitized and cleaned bottles
  • About 50 caps
  • A capper

1. Step One

Dissolve the honey in a little bit of water.

2. Step Two

Use your racking cane and tubing to get the brew out of the carboy and into your really big pot. Add the honey and stir together.

3. Step Three

Siphon the mixture evenly into the bottles. Try to fill them up to the bottom/middlish area of the neck. Then, cap the bottles after you've filled them.

4. Step Four

Put all the bottles somewhere safe and wait another two weeks.
